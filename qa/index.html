Section 01

Checkout, payments, shipping
& tracking

Shopify combines a high-trust checkout with extensibility (Functions, UI extensions, apps) so you can deliver the experience you want, the way you want it—thanks to our flexible building blocks.

Checkout: addresses, quantity rules & customer-facing copy

Address validation and autofill

When details look wrong or incomplete, checkout can surface issues and suggested corrections automatically—cutting down invalid addresses and delivery exceptions. If you turn validation off or an address sits outside supported countries, buyers can still complete checkout; basic field rules (required, format, length) stay on by market.

  • Address collection & validation

Quantity limits by role or SKU

Set min, max, and increment per variant inside B2B catalogs. Assign the catalog to a company location: every contact tied to that location inherits the same limits—ideal for MOQs, case packs, and wholesale guardrails. Rules are enforced through catalog configuration rather than ad hoc per checkout.

  • B2B quantity rules & volume pricing

Custom error messaging

Most online-store wording comes from theme translations and is edited under Settings → Languages (Language Editor). Checkout and system messages are maintained in the same language tools. Shopify Cart and Checkout Validation Functions can be used at checkout to supply custom blocking messages. Checkout Blocks (powered by Functions) can also reorder, rename, or conditionally hide delivery and payment options.

  • Theme wording & translations
  • Checkout Blocks customizations

Payments: split tender, gift cards, store credit & accelerated checkout

Multiple gift cards, store credit & card in one checkout

Customers redeem gift cards on the storefront; store credit can appear at checkout once issued to a signed-in buyer. Multiple gift cards, store credit, and a credit card can combine in a single transaction. Which tenders appear is configured in admin (payment methods and store-credit settings).

  • Redeeming gift cards
  • Store credit

Accelerated / fast pay options

For most stores, turning on accelerated checkouts is as simple as flipping each method to on in Payment methods—no code required for the standard Shop Pay, Apple Pay, Google Pay, Amazon Pay, PayPal, and similar options. Where those buttons surface—product page, cart, or checkout—still depends on your theme and integration settings.

  • Accelerated checkouts

Fraud monitoring & automation

Shopify surfaces fraud signals, risk indicators, and a fulfillment recommendation so you can review suspicious orders before they ship—important for limiting chargebacks on Shopify Payments and other gateways. Shopify Flow adds automation (hold, tag, cancel) using the same risk data. Where eligible, Shopify Protect and standard dispute workflows complement the processor’s controls (for example 3-D Secure, AVS/CVV outcomes). Third-party fraud apps help when you need deeper rules, broader channel coverage, or specialized compliance flows—weigh their cost against native risk plus your payment provider’s tooling.

  • Fraud analysis & protecting orders

Ship-to rules: profiles & zones

Shipping profiles tie products to fulfillment locations and define which zones have active methods—checkout only offers shipping that matches that profile + zone matrix. For harder stops (custom messages, mixed-line rules), extend with checkout customization: Cart/Checkout Validation Functions, Checkout Blocks, or apps.

  • Shipping profiles
  • Customize checkout configurations
  • Checkout Blocks customizations

Shipping rules & carrier-calculated rates

Within each profile and zone you can mix flat, weight- or price-based rates with carrier- or app-calculated options (USPS, UPS, FedEx, DHL, and others via Shopify’s carrier integrations or custom carrier services). That lets you publish table rates domestically while rate-shopping international parcels, for example.

  • DeliveryCarrierService (GraphQL)

Customer-facing tracking & shipping promotions

Order status & tracking

After you add tracking numbers to fulfillments, customers can follow shipments on the order status page, email notifications, and the Shop app (where enabled).

  • Order status page & tracking

Free or promotional shipping

You can reward free shipping either by configuring a $0 shipping option (for example when cart value crosses a threshold on an existing method) or by creating free shipping discounts—manual codes or automatic discounts with eligibility rules (customers, segments, markets, minimums). Product-specific free shipping often uses a dedicated profile with tailored rates.

  • Free shipping on a shipping option
  • Free shipping discounts

Section 02

Coupons, promotions, pricing
& scheduling

How Shopify models discount codes, eligibility, product scope, limits, stacking, and timed promotions—alongside B2B catalogs for list pricing and integrations such as Salsify for product and price data.

Unique codes & discount distribution

Shopify supports amount off (fixed or %), buy X get Y, and free shipping as codes or automatic discounts. Order-level savings apply to the subtotal before tax; tax is calculated on the discounted amount. Codes can be a phrase you choose or a random unique string. Customers redeem codes online at checkout or in person via Shopify POS. Pair native discounts with Shopify Flow to generate alerts, customer journeys, and operational follow-up around codes and campaigns.

  • Discounts overview
  • Manage discount eligibility
  • Discount codes
  • POS discount management

Role- and segment-based coupons

You control which customers can receive a discount by setting eligibility—the same options apply to discount codes and automatic discounts. You can open a promotion to all customers, limit it to customer segments (groups who share characteristics you define—for example first-time buyers via a segment like number_of_orders = 0), or restrict it to specific customers you choose. When you use segments, Shopify can recognize qualifying buyers at checkout when they use a valid email address or phone number.

  • Edit a discount

Include / exclude products & collections

Merchants choose what a discount touches: amount off deals can apply to a line, a collection, or the entire cart. Buy X get Y sets purchase rules and rewards—available as codes or automatic discounts when conditions are met.

  • Discounts
  • Discount types

Usage limits & active dates

Under Maximum discount uses, cap how many times a code can run across the store, and/or limit to one use per customer using verified email or phone. The Active dates block at the bottom of each discount sets the live window—start is required; end is optional.

  • Amount off discounts (limits & scheduling)

Stacking rules (combinations)

Each discount belongs to a class: product (lines/collections), order (subtotal), or shipping. When you allow combinations, you pick which other classes can stack so buyers get the best legitimate total. Multiple product, order, and shipping discounts can apply to one order when their combination rules permit—plan your matrix so finance and CX agree on outcomes.

  • Discount combinations
  • Combination examples

Source of truth for pricing (e.g. Salsify & catalogs)

Pricing often originates in an ERP or PIM and flows into Shopify via integration. Salsify is a common PIM; Shopify provides a Salsify connector path for product information (see Shopify’s enterprise overview). Catalogs hold the published assortment and price for each audience—company locations, markets, or hybrid rules—so list price is authoritative inside Shopify even when the upstream system remains the long-term source of truth.

  • PIM: Shopify & Salsify
  • Catalogs

Scheduled promotions & Launchpad events

Every discount carries its own active date range. For broader seasonal plays, Launchpad (Shopify Plus) can orchestrate the same window across pricing changes, channel product releases, inventory bumps, theme or content swaps, checkout scripts (where used), optional password pages for hype, and event reporting—so merchandising, marketing, and ops move together.

  • Scheduling amount-off discounts
  • Launchpad

Section 03

Pro program, catalogs
& multi-location ops

How list pricing reaches companies and markets, how pro applications move through Forms and Flow, and how inventory, shipping profiles, and order routing work together for omnichannel fulfillment.

Pro pricing (customer groups & price lists)

Assign catalogs to companies and locations so any buyer shopping on behalf of that account sees the correct list prices online and in Shopify POS. You can also assign catalogs at the market level so shoppers in that market see market-specific pricing—whether the motion is account-based wholesale or broader D2C. A catalog is the assortment and prices you publish for a defined audience (for example, visitors browsing from Canada and the United States under one catalog). Markets now support richer segmentation, including submarkets, so you can represent narrower customer or regional slices. POS stays aligned with admin pricing so updates propagate consistently across retail and digital channels.

  • Catalogs in Markets (dev)
  • Market catalogs
  • Markets overview

Pro program applications & approvals

The GovX ID app is one path to verify professional identity at signup. Other identity providers can exchange data with Shopify through APIs, middleware, or connectors; attributes can live in metafields or metaobjects and sync to downstream apps the same way. Shopify Forms ingests account requests with a field layout you control—add or remove inputs to match your underwriting checklist. Shopify Flow layers on top to automate as much or as little as you need: alerts, tagging, company setup, and follow-on Admin API actions.

  • GovX ID (App Store)
  • Shopify Forms
  • Create a form

Multiple physical & virtual locations

Stock products across retail stores, warehouses, dropship apps, and custom fulfillment services at the same time. When a SKU is enabled at multiple locations, inventory is tracked separately per site—quantities are not pooled across locations. Fulfillment follows your order routing and shipping profiles: routing decides where to fulfill, while each shipping profile defines the shipping rules (products, originating locations, and zones/rates) that buyers see at checkout.

  • Multi-managed inventory
  • Order routing
  • Shipping profiles

Role- & priority-based allocation, omni-channel inventory

Order routing is how Shopify applies allocation rules across online, retail, and hybrid journeys: a series of rules evaluates each order, ranks eligible locations, and assigns fulfillment to the best-ranked sites that can service the cart. Built-in defaults already bias toward complete shipments, staying inside the buyer’s market, and using closest stock—if that matches your network, you may not need custom logic. For bespoke prioritization, add rules such as ranked locations or location metafields, and extend behavior with the Order Routing Location Rule on Shopify Functions. Functions let you inject custom backend decisions into checkout, draft orders, edits, imports, and post-checkout surfaces that re-run or read routing outcomes.

Section 04

Analytics, data layer
& Google Tag Manager

Keep measurement stable as you scale: a single data layer contract, GTM on every surface, consent-aware firing for Google and third parties, and rich ecommerce payloads for every product touchpoint.

Objectives: consistent measurement & routing

The goal is to preserve your existing data layer definition—the same event names and parameter shapes shoppers already generate—while covering the full journey: browse and cart stages, abandonment signals, the checkout funnel, and completed purchases, plus merchandising insight such as popular and related products. Google Tag Manager should load on every page template so tags can forward hits to Google Analytics, Google Ads, Meta, and any other vendors that must respect cookie choices. Implementation should favor early, reliable pushes to dataLayer on navigation and interaction so downstream tags read fresh state without race conditions.

High-level & technical requirements

Maintain parity with your current spec: no silent renames or dropped fields. Where events carry an ecommerce object, each line item should include the granular product attributes your dashboards expect (not just SKU and price). Consent changes—first choice and every update—should be observable in the data layer so GTM can gate or reconfigure tags and you retain an audit trail for compliance tooling.

Technical setup your partner handles

Someone on the build side (agency or dev) needs to hook up two kinds of data for tracking to work the way you expect. First: extra product fields you care about—anything beyond the basics—so those values show up where Google Tag Manager and your analytics tools read them. Second: promotional content on the site—things like banners or featured modules—so you can tell when shoppers saw them and when they clicked them. If those aren’t wired in, promotion and product reports can look wrong even when the storefront looks fine.

Interactions to instrument

Align GTM triggers and tag configurations to emit (or pass through) at least the following. Add custom events for experiences such as a system builder or other interactive tools.

  • Purchase on the order confirmation / thank-you experience
  • Cart and checkout funnel steps
  • Add to cart and remove from cart
  • Promotion clicks and promotion impressions (e.g. merchandising banners)
  • view_item, select_item, view_item_list
  • On-site search usage and results
  • Custom events for non-catalog interactions

Native analytics in Shopify admin

Alongside your data layer and GTM work, Shopify provides its own reporting in admin—a separate track from GA4 or Meta pixels, but useful for day-to-day merchandising and performance checks. The Analytics area includes a customizable dashboard made of metric cards (for example net sales by channel and sessions by device type); each card can open a deeper report, and explorations you save can add new cards to the library. Live View shows real-time online store activity when you run a storefront. Staff need the right Analytics permissions to see these views.

This does not replace your client-side event spec: tags that read dataLayer for Google Analytics, Ads, Meta, and other tools still need to be implemented and consent-gated as you’ve outlined above. Where your plan supports it, ShopifyQL also lets you query commerce datasets—such as sales, orders, products, customers, and sessions—for custom analysis inside Shopify’s reporting model.

On the privacy side, Shopify exposes the Customer Privacy API so the storefront knows what processing is allowed (analytics, marketing, preferences, and related signals). That is what third-party consent tools and your GTM rules ultimately need to stay aligned with—covered in more detail in the next section for OneTrust and tag firing.

OneTrust, cookie preferences & GTM

OneTrust (and similar platforms) centralize consent and preference management—cookie banners, category-level choices, and records of what visitors agreed to—so marketing and analytics tags don’t run out of policy. Shopify’s overview of OneTrust highlights how consent can be stored centrally and synced to connected systems, reducing manual spreadsheet work and keeping email, ads, and onsite tracking aligned when someone updates preferences.

For your stack, the implementation pattern is: capture initial and updated consent in the data layer (or Consent Mode–compatible signals), use GTM to condition tag firing on those categories, and only then load or un-block Google Analytics, Ads, Meta pixels, and other third-party scripts. Document the mapping from OneTrust categories → GTM consent variables → each vendor tag so QA can verify opt-in and opt-down paths.

  • What is OneTrust? (Shopify)

GA4, Shopify & the Google sales channel

Google Analytics 4 uses an event-based model: page loads, clicks, adds-to-cart, and purchases are discrete events with parameters, which suits omnichannel shoppers better than older session-only views. In Shopify, wiring GA typically starts with the Google & YouTube channel: it helps create data streams, connect Merchant Center, and surface Google’s measurement tools from admin—complementing whatever you inject through GTM and your custom data layer. Shopify’s Help Center walks through Google Analytics setup for stores; the ecommerce guide below ties those concepts to merchandising and conversion reporting.
